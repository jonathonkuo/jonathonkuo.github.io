Papers

Engaging Middle School Teachers and Students with Alice in a Diverse Set of Subjects

Susan H. Rodger, Jenna Hayes, Gaetjens Lezin, Henry Qin, Deborah Nelson, Ruth Tucker, Mercedes Lopez, Stephen Cooper, Wanda Dann and Don Slater

Citation:
Susan H. Rodger, Jenna Hayes, Gaetjens Lezin, Henry Qin, Deborah Nelson, Ruth Tucker, Mercedes Lopez, Stephen Cooper, Wanda Dann and Don Slater, Engaging Middle School Teachers and Students with Alice in a Diverse Set of Subjects, Fourtieth SIGCSE Technical Symposium on Computer Science Education, p.271-275, 2009. pdf.

Summary:
With middle and high school being such a formative time in people's lives and the tendency to make career decisions in that time, the authors aim to use Alice as a way of exposing computer science as a viable and interesting future to all kinds of people. In order to do this, Alice can be implemented in a multifarious set of subjects, including math, science, language arts, history, art, and technology. Through summer workshops, middle and high school teachers were able to develop lesson plans in all of these subjects. Furthermore, through summer camps, students themselves were exposed to Alice, showing a high level of interest. Following tutorials, students were able to use the programming skills they had learned in order to build their own worlds in their free time. This shows how students are willing to engage in computing if given the opportunity.


Enhancing K-12 Education with Alice Programming Adventures

Susan H. Rodger, Maggie Bashford, Lana Dyck, Jenna Hayes, Liz Liang, Deborah Nelson, and Henry Qin

Citation:
Susan H. Rodger, Maggie Bashford, Lana Dyck, Jenna Hayes, Liz Liang, Deborah Nelson, and Henry Qin, Enhancing K-12 Education with Alice Programming Adventures, The 15th Annual Conference on Innovation and Technology in Computer Science Education (ITiCSE 2010), Ankara, Turkey, p.234-238, 2010. pdf.

Summary:
Due to the large number of students using technology and the low number of students understanding computer science, the authors looked to use Alice as an introduction into computing and animation concepts. By developing materials for teachers and students to use, as well as by working with teachers at workshops, the authors worked to integrate Alice into schools. The authors cite their work in creating tutorials in getting started, animation effects, creating objects, computer science concepts, quizzes, and more. The authors provide examples of Alice worlds centered around the subjects of math and music. For example, an Alice world was created that involved a game where the goal was to identify instruments. The authors have found that there are a multitude of objects that could be useful to teachers that are not available in Alice yet. For instance, a history teacher would be able to utilize objects representing presidents and U.S. government buildings. Working with teachers closely and using their feedback, the authors continue to work to create materials like tutorials and template classes.


Growing a K-12 Community of Practice

Stephen Cooper, Susan H. Rodger, Madeleine Schep, RoxAnn H. Stalvey, Wanda Dann

Citation:
Stephen Cooper, Susan H. Rodger, Madeleine Schep, RoxAnn H. Stalvey, Wanda Dann, Growing a K-12 Community of Practice, Fourty-sixth SIGCSE Technical Symposium on Computer Science Education, Kansas City, Missouri, p. 290-295, 2015. pdf.

Summary:
In this report of experiences, the authors share what they have learned through their attempts to build and grow a community of practice for middle and high school teachers aimed at using Alice to teach basic computational thinking. In contrast to other National Science Foundation Innovative Technology Experiences for Students and Teachers grant winners, the authors were faced with the challenge of integrating a wholly new subject into education. Without existing computer science departments or existing curricula, a community of practice for K-12 teachers was essential for the success of the professional development of teachers in K-12 computer science education. Thus, the authors built an Alice community of practice through professional development workshops in three states, curricular materials available online, teacher-made materials like lesson plans, school-year support, Alice community communication through an e-mail listserv, a symposium to share progress, and 3D Alice model creation to help teachers more effectively integrate the language into their curricula. Through doing this work, the authors learned many important lessons that drastically impact the success of the Alice Project and provide insight into K-12 computer science education. Some of these lessons include: the importance of the location of workshops (hosting workshops appears to be much more effective than travelling to teachers), issues with maintaining school-year interactions with teachers, the usefulness of developing local sub-communities of practice as they can work together to solve problems, the problems with education bureacracy that stand in the way of many teachers, and many more salient points. The authors hope that the sharing of these experiences and lessons learned will help others in their work to build other communities of practice.


Influence of the Familiarization with “Scratch” on Future Teachers’ Opinions and Attitudes about Programming and ICT in Education

Georgios Fesakis and Kiriaki Serafeim

Citation:
Fesakis, Georgios, and Kiriaki Serafeim. "Influence of the familiarization with scratch on future teachers' opinions and attitudes about programming and ICT in education." ACM SIGCSE Bulletin. Vol. 41. No. 3. ACM, 2009. pdf.

Summary:
The authors point out the fact that computer programming is generally difficult to learn and has higher failure rates than other subjects. Because of this, many efforts have been made to increase attendance and decrease the difficulty of introductory programming education. One such effort has been the development of user-friendly programming environments, like Alice and Scratch. The authors describe Scratch, a project of MIT Media Lab, pointing out its drag-and-drop structure, integration of computational concepts, large network of users, and aesthetic design. To learn more about the effects of Scratch, the authors performed a study to test the effect of the level of familiarity with Scratch on the opinions and attitudes of future teachers on Information and Communication Technologies (ICT). To do so, pre-education students of the University of the Aegean (Greece) were exposed to Scratch through a mandatory course and were given surveys before and after. The initial survey showed little experience in computer programming within the group, and a relatively high level of anxiety about the subject. Despite this, the initial survey also showed that these students were interested in using ICT in education. The final survey showed that familiarity with a user-friendly programming environment like Scratch increased the interest in using ICT in education and significantly decreased the stress and anxiety regarding their ability to utilize ICT in education. This information confirms and validates the decision to use Scratch in an introductory computer programming course.


Integrating Computing into Middle School Disciplines Through Projects

Susan Rodger, Melissa Dalis, Chitra Gadwal, Jenna Hayes, Peggy Li, Liz Liang, Francine Wolfe, and Wenhui Zhang

Citation:
Susan Rodger, Melissa Dalis, Chitra Gadwal, Jenna Hayes, Peggy Li, Liz Liang, Francine Wolfe, and Wenhui Zhang, Integrating Computing into Middle Schools Disciplines Through Projects, Fourty-third SIGCSE Technical Symposium on Computer Science Education, Raleigh, NC, p. 421-426, 2012. pdf.

Summary:
With middle schoolers' low amount of exposure to computer science, the authors developed Alice-based curriculum materials for teachers. These materials can be used to introduce students to basic programming and animation concepts. The authors describe in detail specific projects that have been developed for science and math classrooms, but also emphasize the potential for Alice in other disciplines like history and foreign language. For science, Alice can be used to model experiments to visualize phenomena like overfishing as well as to view microscopic subjects. For math, the authors have found a multitude of applications for Alice, including functions, points, percentages, inequalities, sampling, and more. The authors conclude with a few statistics collected from their workshops, showing that math teachers generally showed the highest aptitude for programming, while technology teachers showed the most interest. Additionally, projects that involved an interactive game introduced the highest number of Alice/programming concepts compared to projects that had students create their own worlds and presentation-only projects. The authors continue to create Alice tutorials and projects to further the work of integrating computing into middle school education.


Weaving Computing into all Middle School Disciplines

Susan H. Rodger, Dwayne Brown, Michael Hoyle, Daniel MacDonald, Michael Marion, Elizabeth Onstwedder, Bella Onwumbiko, and Edwin Ward

Citation:
Susan H. Rodger, Dwayne Brown, Michael Hoyle, Daniel MacDonald, Michael Marion, Elizabeth Onstwedder, Bella Onwumbiko, and Edwin Ward, Weaving Computing into all Middle School Disciplines, The 19th Annual Conference on Innovation and Technology in Computer Science Education (ITiCSE 2014), p. 207-212 Uppsala, Sweden, 2014. pdf.

Summary:
Given the deficiency of computer science education in American middle schools, the authors worked to teach middle school teachers of a wide variety of disciplines Alice in an attempt to integrate computing into their classes. The authors describe their work with teachers, which includes workshops, materials, and outreach. In the two-week summer workshops, middle school teachers are exposed to introductory and topical tutorials in order to become familiar with the programming environment. Additionally, the workshops introduce teachers to helper objects that can help teachers and students to develop animations more effectively; one example of a helper object is a quiz object that helps to assess understanding. Furthermore, teachers can give their students Alice challenges, wherein the students are given a world with a few missing pieces, giving them an opportunity to program and develop their understanding of class materials. Finally, the authors provide sample projects and worlds online that could serve as useful tools in the classroom. These could ask a science student to construct the solar system or a math student to demonstrate her understanding of the coordinate system. An important outcome of the workshops are teacher-created lesson plans that clearly outline how an Alice world can be used for a certain concept. Altogether, these materials are available for free online for teachers and students to access. Moreover, the authors have worked with students in the classroom, who showed an aptitude and an interest in computing, showing the importance of the work. Through curriculum materials and proffessional development of teachers, the authors (and teachers) are continuing to work on integrating computer science into K-12 education.
